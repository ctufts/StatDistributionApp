Beta Distribution

Overview

The beta distribution provides an probability distribution of a probability of an event with 2 possible outcomes. I'll use estimating if a coin is fair as an example. (If you are tired of the old coin flip examples for everything in statistics please check out this batting average example by David Robinson) To illustrate the coin flip example, try out the following:

  • Start at a = 1 and b = 1. Think of this as 1 head (a) and 1 tails(b). We can see at this point the probability of the coin being fair, x = 0.5, is equivalent to the probability of x = 0.1 or 0.9 or any other value. The beta distribution is effectively telling us we don't have enough information to get a good estimate of the 'fairness' of the coin.
  • Increase the number of heads (a) to 10 and leave b set to 1. Note that the probability of the coin being fair is very low, but the probability of the coin being biased towards heads (x > 0.5) is much higher.
  • Slide the Beta parameter to a value of 10 (10 heads 10 tails). In this case the distribution is centered over the value of x = 0.5. This would lead us to believe the the coin is reasonably fair based on the evidence provided.
  • Set alpha and beta parameters to 20. This would signify 20 heads and 20 tails which would be fairly strong evidence that the coin is fair. Note that the distribution is considerably narrower with more evidence present

Parameters

  • beta: number of outcome #1
    • # of coin flips with heads as an outcome
  • alpha: number of outcome #2
    • # of coin flips with tails as an outcome
  • Both are shape parameters

Interesting Properties

    • mode = 0.5
    • mean = 0.5
  • spread of data is related to concentration, k
    • k = a + b
    • Distribution shape narrows as k increases
